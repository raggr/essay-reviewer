The Unreliable Narrator as Structural Principle in Kazuo Ishiguro's The Remains of the Day

Kazuo Ishiguro's The Remains of the Day is frequently read as a novel about English repression — a butler who cannot express emotion, set against a backdrop of aristocratic decline. This reading, while not wrong, flattens the novel's most interesting structural achievement: its use of unreliable narration not as a psychological quirk but as a formal principle that organises the entire text.

Stevens does not simply fail to see what the reader sees. His narration actively constructs an alternative reality in which professional excellence substitutes for moral agency. When he describes his father's famous anecdote about the butler who discovered a tiger under the dining table and served dinner without mentioning it, Stevens presents this as the pinnacle of "dignity." But the novel's structure ensures that this anecdote sits adjacent to scenes in which Stevens's own failure to mention things — his employer's Nazi sympathies, his feelings for Miss Kenton — carries consequences the tiger story does not. The juxtaposition is not accidental. Ishiguro builds a formal architecture in which Stevens's aesthetic of discretion is tested against situations where discretion becomes complicity.

What makes this more than a character study is the temporal structure. The novel alternates between Stevens's present-day road trip and his memories of Darlington Hall in the 1930s. In the present-day sections, Stevens is relatively self-aware: he acknowledges mistakes, questions his choices, even attempts humour. In the memory sections, he retreats into the language of professional duty. The effect is that the novel contains two narrators — or rather, one narrator at two different distances from self-knowledge. The reader must navigate between them, and the gap between them is where the novel's meaning lives.

This dual narrator structure complicates the common reading of Stevens as simply "in denial." He is not unaware of what happened at Darlington Hall; the present-day sections make clear that he has processed, at least partially, the moral failures of his employer and his own role in enabling them. What he cannot do — what the novel's structure forbids him from doing until the final pages — is integrate this knowledge into a coherent account of his own life. The two temporal registers remain separate, and the novel's tragedy is precisely their failure to merge.

The famous scene at the end of the novel, where Stevens weeps on the pier at Weymouth, has been read as his "breakthrough moment." But this reading is too redemptive. Stevens weeps, but he then immediately returns to the language of professional improvement — he resolves to practice his "bantering" so that he can better serve his new American employer. The structure of the novel does not permit genuine resolution. It offers recognition without transformation, awareness without the capacity to act on it. This is not a failure of the character but an achievement of the form: Ishiguro has constructed a narrative voice that can see its own limitations but cannot transcend them.

One limitation of this reading is that it privileges formal structure over historical context. The novel is deeply engaged with the politics of appeasement, and readings that focus exclusively on narratological technique risk losing the specific moral weight of Stevens's complicity with fascism. The formal reading and the historical reading need each other: the unreliable narration matters because of what it conceals, and what it conceals matters because of what it enabled. A purely formalist account of the novel would be as incomplete as a purely historicist one.

Nevertheless, attending to the structural principle of unreliable narration reveals something that thematic readings often miss: the novel is not simply about a man who cannot face the truth. It is about the formal conditions under which self-knowledge becomes possible — and the devastating suggestion that those conditions may never be fully met. Stevens's narration is not a veil to be lifted but a structure to be inhabited, and the reader's experience of inhabiting it is the novel's deepest argument about the relationship between language, consciousness, and moral agency.
